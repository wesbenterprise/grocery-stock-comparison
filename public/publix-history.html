Revenue & Adjusted Net Income

Quarterly revenue (bars) and operating net income ex. securities G/L (line) with key event annotations
◆ Anderson's Take
Revenue has compounded at 6.8% annually over the decade — steady, not spectacular. The story is really in operating earnings (stripped of unrealized securities G/L): operating net income CAGR of ~8.5% means Publix has consistently grown the bottom line faster than the top through actual business operations. The 2022 earnings compression despite accelerating revenue is the single most important chart on this page — it shows exactly how inflation squeezed margins even as customers kept spending. Post-2022 recovery has been strong on an operating basis.

Margin Analysis

Gross, operating & net margins with industry reference lines
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix runs 2–4x the operating margins of the typical U.S. grocery chain. The moat is not pricing power — it's cost structure. SG&A efficiency and labor productivity drive the spread. The secular gross margin decline (28.7% → 25.3% over 10 years) is a quiet warning sign: if it continues without offsetting SG&A cuts, operating margins follow. Watch this line. The 2022–2023 compression was inflation-driven and has partially recovered; the underlying downtrend predates it.

Balance Sheet

Total assets, liabilities & stockholders' equity over time
◆ Anderson's Take
The 2019 liability spike looks alarming but is purely accounting: ASC 842 required operating leases onto the balance sheet for the first time. Publix has essentially no traditional debt. The equity growth story (CAGR +10.3%) is clean and consistent — a company that funds everything internally and still compounds equity at double digits is a rare thing in low-margin retail.

Store Expansion

Store count growth with net new stores per year
◆ Anderson's Take
From 1,114 stores in 2015 to 1,432 in 2025 — 29% growth in footprint over a decade. Notably, revenue per store grew faster than store count: the fleet is getting more productive, not just bigger. New stores are opening into markets where Publix's brand already has pricing power. The question for the next decade is whether that productivity premium holds as they push into less-established markets.

Operating Cash Flow, Capex & Free Cash Flow

Quarterly cash generation and capital deployment
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix generates cash at a rate that most grocers can't match. Annual OCF has grown from ~$3.5B in FY2015 to over $8B by FY2025 — more than doubling in a decade. The OCF-to-net-income spread is consistently wide because D&A is a real non-cash charge running $550M+ per quarter by 2025, and working capital is favorable (suppliers effectively float Publix's inventory). Free cash flow tells the real story: after $2B+ in annual capex for store construction and remodels, Publix still generates $3–4B of FCF per year. That's not a grocery company. That's a cash machine wearing a grocery uniform.

Capital Allocation

How Publix deploys capital — capex, dividends, buybacks, and retained
◆ Anderson's Take
The capital allocation picture reveals a disciplined, internally-funded growth machine. Capex runs $2–2.5B annually — almost entirely new stores, remodels, and technology — and is covered several times over by operating cash flow. Dividends have grown steadily and reflect the employee-ownership model: Publix associates are the shareholders, so the dividend policy doubles as a profit-sharing mechanism. Buybacks are opportunistic and modest relative to the cash pile. The purple "retained" bar is the most telling — in most years, Publix retains $1B+ after all distributions, compounding equity organically at double-digit rates without touching debt markets. When the retained bar dips negative, it's a deliberate choice to return more than was generated that quarter — a signal of confidence, not distress.

Cash Conversion

Operating cash flow and free cash flow as % of revenue
◆ Anderson's Take
OCF conversion — cash from operations as a percentage of revenue — runs 7–9% in normal years, which is exceptional for a grocery business where 2–3% is typical. The FCF margin (after capex) sits at 4–6% consistently. Two things drive this spread above peers: favorable supplier payment terms that create a structural working capital advantage, and a real estate strategy (mostly owned or long-term leased) that front-loads capex but eliminates rent escalation risk. The 2020 spike in OCF conversion was genuine — COVID demand surge hit margins without proportional cost increases. The subsequent normalization back to the trend line is exactly what you'd expect from a well-managed operation. The trend line itself is what matters: it hasn't compressed. That's a durable moat signal.

Equity Compounding

Total stockholders' equity and book value per share
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix has grown book equity from roughly $8.6B in 2015 to over $26B by 2025 — a ~11.6% CAGR in absolute terms. But that understates the per-shareholder story. Book value per share has compounded faster over the same period, the difference attributable almost entirely to a sustained buyback program that has retired roughly 17% of split-adjusted float. Equity isn't just growing — it's concentrating. Each remaining share commands a larger claim on the balance sheet than it did a decade ago, and that gap widens each year repurchases continue.

Share Count & Buybacks

Split-adjusted shares outstanding and quarterly buyback spend (pre-2022 figures ×5 for split)
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix has been a consistent repurchaser. On a split-adjusted basis, shares outstanding have declined from approximately 3,851M in 2015 to roughly 3,216M by 2025 — a reduction of ~16.5% over a decade, retiring roughly 60–70M shares per year on average. Publix is private; these repurchases represent buybacks from departing associates and estate transactions, not open-market buying. The mechanical effect on per-share metrics is identical to a public company buyback. When buyback spend spikes relative to share reduction, the implicit price paid per share rose — Publix was buying at higher valuations.

EPS vs Adj. Net Income — Indexed Growth

Both rebased to 100 at 2015-Q1. The gap is the buyback accretion premium.
◆ Anderson's Take
Operating net income has grown significantly since 2015, but split-adjusted EPS has grown faster. The spread between the two indexed lines is the mechanical output of ten years of share count reduction. Publix hasn't just grown earnings — it's grown earnings per remaining share faster than earnings grew in absolute terms. For an associate-owned company where the shareholders are employees, this compounds directly in their favor: each associate's stake earns more not just because the business is more profitable, but because there are fewer shares dividing that profit.

Shareholder Returns Composition

Dividends and buybacks vs operating net income — Capital Return Rate
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix returns capital through both dividends and buybacks, and the Capital Return Rate — total returns as a percentage of operating net income — tells the real story about management's capital philosophy. In years where it exceeds 100%, Publix is returning more than it earned: drawing on operating cash flow above the net income line. This is not financial stress — OCF consistently exceeds net income due to D&A and working capital dynamics. It is a deliberate signal: Publix is not building a war chest. It is returning capital to its associates. The consistency of that commitment, year after year, is the policy.

Asset Composition

Operating assets vs. investment portfolio — quarterly FY2015–FY2025
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix's balance sheet is not a grocery balance sheet. The physical store footprint — PP&E — is roughly $15B, but sitting alongside it is a $20B investment portfolio that has nothing to do with selling groceries. Long-term investments have compounded at roughly 9–10% annually since 2015. The ROU assets that appeared in 2019 reflect ASC 842 lease capitalization, not a real change in the business — Publix has always leased most of its locations. What you're looking at is a company that generates so much cash it can fund its own store growth AND accumulate an investment portfolio larger than the entire market cap of most regional grocery chains.

Balance Sheet Growth

Total assets, equity, and liabilities — quarterly FY2015–FY2025
◆ Anderson's Take
Total assets have grown from roughly $16B in 2015 to over $40B in 2025 — a 2.5x increase in a decade, compounding at roughly 10% annually. The notable jump in liabilities in 2019 is entirely ASC 842 lease capitalization — Publix didn't take on debt, they just started reporting leases on the balance sheet as required. Actual financial liabilities remain minimal. Equity has grown in near-lockstep with assets, which tells you everything: this company funds its own growth without borrowing.

Asset Efficiency & Working Capital

Asset turnover (TTM) and current ratio — quarterly FY2015–FY2025
◆ Anderson's Take
Two signals worth separating. Asset turnover has drifted down from ~1.1x in 2015 to roughly 0.7x — not because the stores got less efficient, but because the investment portfolio keeps growing faster than revenue. If you stripped out the $20B investment portfolio and measured turnover against operating assets only, you'd see a highly efficient retailer. The current ratio below 1.0x is deliberate and healthy: Publix collects cash at the register before it pays vendors, which means the business self-funds its working capital needs. A current ratio below 1.0 for Publix is a sign of operational leverage, not distress.

Liability Structure

What Publix owes — and what it doesn't
◆ Anderson's Take
Publix's liabilities are almost entirely operating in nature — vendor payables and lease obligations. The AP balance is float: Publix sells groceries for cash before it has to pay the producers. The lease stack grew significantly in 2019 but that's ASC 842 bringing long-standing operating leases onto the balance sheet for the first time — it's the same stores, same leases, just now visible. The deferred tax liability is ~$1.6B, which is accounting timing, not a real cash claim in any near-term window. Long-term debt is functionally zero. For a company with $40B in assets, this is an extraordinary capital structure — one that only works when you generate enough free cash flow to never need outside financing.

◆ About This Space
"Mr. George" — George W. Jenkins — opened the first Publix on September 6, 1930 in Winter Haven, Florida, with $1,300 in savings and a conviction that a grocery store could treat people right. His Lakeland office became the operational heart of an empire built on that belief. This 3D walkthrough preserves the space as he left it: the desk, the mementos, the evidence of sixty years spent building something that outlasted him. Every Publix associate today works in the shadow of what happened in this room.
